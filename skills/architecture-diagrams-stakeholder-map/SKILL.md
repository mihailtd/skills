# Stakeholder Map — Stakeholder Classification and Deliverable Alignment

A Stakeholder Map is a tabular architecture artifact that identifies key
stakeholders, their power/interest classification, their primary concerns, and
the architecture views or deliverables they require.

This skill is for architects who need to ensure the architecture effort aligns
with the needs of decision makers, influencers, process owners, and external
partners.

---

## When to use this skill

Use this skill when you need to:

- Identify the people and groups affected by or influencing the architecture.
- Understand stakeholder concerns and influence levels.
- Align stakeholders with the architecture deliverables they need.
- Support communication planning and governance.
- Track formal and informal influencers across the enterprise.

---

## Outcome

Produce a Stakeholder Map that shows:

- **Stakeholder**: the individual, group, role, or organization.
- **Key concerns**: the questions, needs, and expectations important to that
  stakeholder.
- **Class**: the stakeholder classification from a power/interest grid, such as
  Key players, Keep satisfied, Keep informed, or Minimal effort.
- **Catalogs, matrices, and diagrams**: the architecture deliverables that will
  answer the stakeholder’s concerns.

The map should be organized as a clean table that makes stakeholder needs and
output requirements visible at a glance.

---

## Step-by-step workflow

1. **Gather stakeholder identities**
   - List individuals, teams, departments, external partners, and informal
     influencers who have a stake in the architecture.
   - Include decision makers, subject matter experts, and those affected by the
     change.

2. **Assess power and interest**
   - Classify stakeholders using a power/interest grid.
   - Use standard categories like Key players, Keep satisfied, Keep informed, and
     Minimal effort.

3. **Capture key concerns**
   - Interview stakeholders or collect input through workshops and surveys.
   - Ask what matters most to them and what questions they need the architecture
     to answer.
   - Record their concerns in business terms.

4. **Map deliverables to stakeholders**
   - Identify which architecture artifacts address each stakeholder’s concerns.
   - Reference specific catalogs, matrices, and diagrams, such as Strategy/Goal,
     Goal/Objective, Business Roles Map, or Business Process Map.

5. **Build the tabular map**
   - Use a table with Stakeholder, Key concerns, Class, and Deliverables columns.
   - Keep the presentation clean and easy to scan.

6. **Review and maintain**
   - Validate the map with stakeholders and senior sponsors.
   - Update it as stakeholder influence, concerns, or required deliverables
     change over time.

---

## Decision points and guidance

- **Stakeholder granularity**: represent individuals or groups depending on the
  level of detail needed; use groups when multiple people share the same concerns
  and classifications.
- **Class selection**: choose classifications based on the stakeholder’s influence
  and level of interest in the architecture outcomes.
- **Concern framing**: frame concerns as the stakeholder’s real questions, not
  generic architecture terms.
- **Deliverable mapping**: be specific about which artifact answers each concern.
  For example, link a CxO to a Strategy/Goal Matrix and a process owner to a
  Business Process Map.

---

## Quality criteria

A strong Stakeholder Map should be:

- **Complete**: includes all relevant internal and external stakeholders.
- **Accurate**: reflects each stakeholder’s true level of influence and concern.
- **Actionable**: shows exactly which deliverables serve each stakeholder.
- **Readable**: uses a simple table and avoids unnecessary detail.
- **Maintained**: updated as stakeholder priorities and architecture needs evolve.
