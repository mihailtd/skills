# Product Backlog Refinement

This skill helps AI explain how Product Backlog Refinement works in a BDD context, how it connects strategic discovery to release planning, and how teams should decompose and prioritize backlog items collaboratively.

## When to use this skill

Use this skill when you need to:

- describe the role of Product Backlog Refinement in agile planning,
- explain how high-level features become refined backlog items,
- show how BDD supports backlog refinement with examples and acceptance criteria,
- help teams decide what to prepare before sprint planning,
- recommend habits for continuous backlog hygiene.

## Outcome

Produce guidance that:

- positions Product Backlog Refinement as an ongoing activity, not a one-time event,
- explains how strategic discovery outputs feed the backlog,
- shows how features are estimated, ordered, and clarified before implementation,
- recommends collaborative refinement with product owners, developers, and testers,
- distinguishes between backlog refinement, release planning, and sprint planning.

## Instructions for the AI

1. **Explain the purpose of refinement**
   - Describe it as the act of adding detail, estimates, and order to backlog items.
   - Mention the Scrum guide definition and how BDD teams use it to prepare features for the next release.
   - Emphasize that it reduces uncertainty before teams commit to work.

2. **Connect refinement to BDD discovery**
   - State that backlog items should originate from strategic activities such as Impact Mapping and Pirate Canvases.
   - Explain that refinement is where teams translate those high-level deliverables into backlog-ready features and stories.
   - Emphasize business goals, capabilities, and hypothesis-driven thinking.

3. **Describe refinement practices**
   - Recommend splitting large features into smaller, testable pieces.
   - Recommend identifying key examples, acceptance criteria, and success measures.
   - Advise on simple estimates and relative sizing rather than false precision.

4. **Encourage collaboration**
   - Recommend that the product owner, delivery team, and testers participate.
   - Explain that team members add technical insight, risks, and dependencies.
   - Suggest inviting stakeholders or delegates for larger-scale refinement.

5. **Warn against over-refinement**
   - Explain that too much detail too early wastes effort.
   - Advise keeping refinement light enough to stay adaptable.
   - Recommend revisiting and reprioritizing backlog items regularly as new information arrives.

## Decision points and guidance

- **If the backlog item is too large:** recommend splitting it into smaller features or stories.
- **If the item is too vague:** recommend capturing a clear business goal and at least one example.
- **If the team lacks confidence:** suggest identifying the assumptions and risks that need discovery.
- **If the backlog is too cluttered:** recommend removing or archiving items that no longer align with business goals.

## Quality criteria

A strong Product Backlog Refinement response should be:

- **preparatory:** it helps teams get work ready for implementation,
- **collaborative:** it keeps product and delivery perspectives together,
- **value-focused:** it links backlog items to business outcomes,
- **lightweight:** it avoids excessive upfront detail,
- **iterative:** it treats the backlog as a living, evolving artifact.
