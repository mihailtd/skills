# Transformative Verbs (Verbs as State of Being)

## Goal
Transform writing by redefining subjects not as static "things" or rigid noun categories, but as dynamic, evolving processes (verbs). Inspired by Ulysses Grant's realization ("I am a verb... to be; to do; or to suffer"), Buckminster Fuller's philosophy, and Cherokee linguistics, this editor uses verbs to express interconnectedness, constant change, and the deep relationship between a subject and its environment.

## Checklist for Writing Review
- [ ] **Replace Static Identities with Evolving Processes**: Check if the subject is locked into a fixed noun category ("He is a leader", "I am a failure"). Rewrite to express their identity as an ongoing action or system of change ("He leads," "I process setbacks").
- [ ] **Capture the "To Suffer" State**: Ensure the writing reflects how a subject endures, absorbs, or is acted upon by their environment (the archaic meaning of "suffer"). Balance proactive verbs (doing) with receptive verbs (experiencing, enduring, yielding).
- [ ] **Highlight Interconnectedness (The Cherokee "Circle")**: Scan descriptions to ensure the subject is not isolated. Use verbs that bind the subject to its surroundings, showing the network of relationships between the person, the setting, and other actors.
- [ ] **Focus on Fluidity and Change**: Replace verbs of permanence (is, was, exists) with verbs that capture fluid states and the impermanence of the human experience.

## Anti-patterns (What to Avoid)
- **Categorizing the Subject**: Reducing a person or concept to a static label or noun without exploring what they *do* or *experience*.
  - *Bad*: "Ulysses S. Grant was a great hero and a suffering cancer patient." / "I am a manager."
- **Isolation/Separation**: Describing an object or person completely independently of the world interacting with them.
  - *Bad*: "The tree stood in the forest." / "She studied the problem." (Lacks connection to the surrounding environment or holistic understanding).

## Good Patterns (What to Do)
- **Verb as Metaphysical Essence**: Describing a person's life as a complete set of actions and endurances.
  - *Good*: "To Ulysses Grant a life means: to set yourself to the task no matter the conditions, to write your story on your own terms, and to persist."
- **Receptive/Experiential Verbs**: Acknowledging how the world acts upon the subject.
  - *Good*: "He absorbed the losses, weathered the harsh landscape, and yielded to the passage of time."
- **Verbs of Holistic Connection**: Action words that pull multiple elements into one closed, connected system.
  - *Good*: "She circled the subject, learning the bark, the roots, and the bugs crawling upon it, weaving herself into the network of the woods."

## Examples

### Example 1: Moving from Static Identity to Process
**Original:** She was an innovator and a problem solver. She was not the type of person to give up.
**Edited:** She innovated. She solved. Like a verb, she functioned as an evolutionary process, refusing to succumb.

### Example 2: Capturing "Suffering" (Being Acted Upon) and Endurance
**Original:** He was devastated by his financial losses and his cancer diagnosis, but he kept writing his book.
**Edited:** He suffered the swindlers and endured the ravages of cancer, yet he gritted his teeth, seized his pencil, and wrote his story on his own terms.

### Example 3: Expressing Interconnectedness
**Original:** The scientist analyzed the environment.
**Edited:** The scientist circled the ecosystem, moving with the water, the roots, and the birds to understand her place within the woods.
