# Pirate Canvas

This skill helps AI explain how Pirate Canvases use the AARRR framework to discover the most valuable opportunities, uncover assumptions, and create a broad Epic Landscape for strategic planning.

## When to use this skill

Use this skill when you need to:

- explore ecosystem-level opportunities and constraints,
- identify the most important bottlenecks across acquisition, activation, retention, referral, and return,
- use Pirate Metrics to structure strategic discovery,
- build an Epic Landscape of candidate deliverables,
- surface whether work should be software, service, or ecosystem-enabled.

## Outcome

Produce guidance that:

- explains Pirate Metrics (Acquisition, Activation, Retention, Referral, Return),
- shows how to frame discovery around “what sucks?” for each metric,
- teaches how to convert problems into measurable goals and deliverables,
- encourages teams to think broadly about actors, outcomes, and ecosystem impact,
- explains how to create an Epic Landscape of high-level deliverables.

## Instructions for the AI

1. **Define Pirate Metrics clearly**
   - Acquisition: bringing actors into the ecosystem.
   - Activation: getting them to see value and engage.
   - Retention: getting them to return.
   - Referral: motivating them to invite others.
   - Return: delivering tangible business benefit.

2. **Explain the Pirate Canvas workshop approach**
   - Recommend starting with a pain point: “What sucks?”
   - Describe the workshop as a breadth-first exploration, not a feature checklist.
   - Show how each metric becomes a column of problems, assumptions, and metrics.

3. **Show how to build the Epic Landscape**
   - Explain how deliverables become epics on the canvas.
   - Emphasize that the most valuable outputs are the ones that remove key bottlenecks.
   - Encourage the team to keep the landscape focused and to discard low-value or unverifiable ideas.

4. **Use examples to make it concrete**
   - Illustrate how a business travel or loyalty example can generate goals like more business flyers, productive conversations, or rebooking.
   - Show how a canvas can surface non-software solutions, such as better customer onboarding or partner incentives.
   - Mention that the canvas can be used with both product and service ecosystems.

5. **Emphasize experimentation and assumptions**
   - Recommend attaching metrics to the chosen goals and deliverables.
   - Encourage teams to identify the riskiest assumptions and test them quickly.
   - Explain that the canvas should be updated as evidence comes in.

6. **Advise on prioritization and focus**
   - Warn against trying to solve every metric at once.
   - Recommend choosing the most constrained metric or the one with the highest leverage.
   - Suggest using the canvas to generate objective discussions about what to do first.

## Decision points and guidance

- **If the user has a feature backlog but no strategy:** use the Pirate Canvas to expose ecosystem constraints.
- **If the team is stuck on product discovery:** focus on the “What sucks?” question for each Pirate Metric.
- **If stakeholders want quick wins:** recommend identifying the highest-impact bottleneck and building a small experiment around it.
- **If the canvas becomes too broad:** recommend narrowing to the metric or actor with the most direct link to business goals.

## Quality criteria

A strong Pirate Canvas response should be:

- **ecosystem-aware:** it considers actors beyond just end users,
- **metric-driven:** it ties ideas to Acquisition/Activation/Retention/Referral/Return,
- **assumption-explicit:** it makes the riskiest bets visible,
- **epic-focused:** it surfaces high-level deliverables rather than detailed stories,
- **adaptive:** it treats the canvas as an evolving learning artifact.
