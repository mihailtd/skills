# Goal/Initiative Diagram — Strategy-to-Initiative Execution View

A Goal/Initiative Diagram visually connects high-level goals to the specific
initiatives or work packages required to achieve them. It shows how strategy
translates into concrete execution, providing a clear path from intent to action.

This skill is for architects who need to make the strategy-to-execution link
transparent and understandable for senior management and delivery teams.

---

## When to use this skill

Use this skill when you need to:

- Show which specific initiatives support each strategic goal.
- Validate that initiatives are aligned with organizational strategy.
- Clarify how execution-level work packages map back to business goals.
- Communicate the strategy-to-change relationship visually.
- Identify gaps between strategy and planned action.

---

## Outcome

Produce a Goal/Initiative Diagram that shows:

- **Goals**: the high-level strategic outcomes the organization seeks.
- **Initiatives / Work Packages**: the concrete projects, programs, or actions
  that will implement the strategy.
- **Relationships**: the explicit links showing which initiatives support which
  goals.
- **Sponsors / Owners**: optionally, the business units or roles responsible for
  the initiatives.

The diagram should make it easy to trace any initiative back to the strategic
goal it serves.

---

## Step-by-step workflow

1. **Capture strategic goals**
   - Gather the organization’s primary goals from strategy documents and
     executive interviews.
   - Ensure goals are outcome-oriented and clearly stated.

2. **Identify initiatives**
   - List the work packages, projects, programs, and activities planned to
     execute the strategy.
   - Work with program owners and business leaders to confirm the initiative
     scope.

3. **Map goals to initiatives**
   - Connect each initiative to the goal or goals it supports.
   - Use the Goal/Initiative Diagram to show these relationships visually.

4. **Add ownership and sponsorship**
   - Where useful, annotate initiatives with the responsible business unit,
     role, or sponsor.
   - This clarifies who is accountable for delivering the work.

5. **Build the diagram**
   - Place strategic goals at the top or one side of the layout.
   - Place initiatives below or opposite the goals.
   - Draw lines, arrows, or hierarchical connections to show support paths.
   - Optionally include objectives as an intermediate layer when needed.

6. **Review and refine**
   - Validate the diagram with senior leadership and initiative owners.
   - Adjust connections, remove unsupported initiatives, and add missing goals.
   - Use the diagram to identify execution gaps or misaligned initiatives.

---

## Decision points and guidance

- **Diagram shape**: use a tree-like hierarchical layout for clarity, with goals at the top and initiatives beneath.
- **Objective layer**: include objectives only if the organization needs a more detailed
  strategy-to-execution path.
- **Ownership detail**: add business roles or units when accountability must be
  visible; omit if it makes the diagram too cluttered.
- **Visual simplification**: avoid overwhelming the diagram with too many
  initiatives; focus on the most important work packages.
- **Relationship clarity**: draw only meaningful links; too many cross-connections
  reduce readability.

---

## Quality criteria

A strong Goal/Initiative Diagram should be:

- **Strategically aligned**: initiatives clearly support the stated goals.
- **Readable**: structured so stakeholders can trace links easily.
- **Action-oriented**: shows execution work, not just abstract strategy.
- **Valid**: confirmed by sponsors and program owners.
- **Maintained**: updated as initiatives evolve and strategy changes.
