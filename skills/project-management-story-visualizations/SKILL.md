# Story Visualizations and Shared Understanding

This skill helps AI explain how to use story cards as tokens, support conversations
with visual artifacts, and choose the right visualization style for different
stakeholders and stages of project work.

---

## When to use this skill

Use this skill when you need to:

- explain why a card is only the beginning of the story,
- help different roles collaborate using shared visual artifacts,
- choose the right visualization style for planning, discussion, validation, and tracking,
- balance face-to-face physical artifacts with digital tools,
- keep information alive and accessible rather than buried in an icebox.

---

## Outcome

Produce guidance that:

- distinguishes story cards as lightweight tokens from the richer information held elsewhere,
- describes how different stakeholders use the same story differently,
- recommends visualization styles for story discovery, detail work, and progress tracking,
- explains when to use walls, whiteboards, diagrams, and tools,
- encourages capturing vacation photos, board snapshots, and lightweight documentation.

---

## Instructions for the AI

1. **Explain the card metaphor**
   - Describe cards as index cards in a catalog: easy to find and organize, but not the full book.
   - Recommend using a short title on the card and keeping other details in associated artifacts.
   - Emphasize that cards are tokens for conversation, not comprehensive specifications.

2. **Describe different stakeholder conversations**
   - Product people: focus on market, outcomes, value, and viability.
   - Business analysts: focus on UI details, business rules, and process flows.
   - Testers: focus on failure modes, edge cases, and acceptance criteria.
   - Designers: focus on users, context, and usable interfaces.
   - Project managers: focus on dependencies, timing, and coordination.
   - Explain that the same story supports all of these conversations through different visual artifacts.

3. **Recommend visualization styles**
   - Story maps: big-picture flow and backbone for release planning.
   - Task/activity boards: user task flow, decisions, and alternatives.
   - Card catalogs: metadata views showing title, status, estimates, dependencies, and metrics.
   - Information radiators: wall charts, Kanban radiators, dashboards, and progress charts.
   - Risk views: maps annotated with questions, unknowns, and risk stories.
   - Design sketches: low-fidelity UI sketches and workflow diagrams.
   - Confirmation views: acceptance criteria and validation checklists.

4. **Support remote and digital collaboration**
   - Recommend physical walls for collocated teams and video/document cameras for remote participants.
   - Advise using digital whiteboards or collaboration tools that let everyone see and edit the same model.
   - Suggest synchronous and asynchronous capture: shared boards, tool updates, and vacation photos.
   - Explain the difference between live collaboration tools and tracking tools.

5. **Explain tool roles clearly**
   - Use physical tools and whiteboards for shared understanding and active conversation.
   - Use digital tools for recording, tracking, searching, and auditing progress.
   - Warn against using tracking tools as a substitute for live collaboration.
   - Encourage using snapshots, photos, and videos to preserve the context of discussions.

6. **Show how to preserve and evolve artifacts**
   - Recommend photographing boards after a session and storing images in a wiki or shared folder.
   - Advise capturing short walkthrough videos for later review.
   - Suggest linking cards to design sketches, acceptance criteria, and conversation notes.
   - Explain that artifacts evolve: the card stays small while the supporting documentation grows.

---

## Decision points and guidance

- **Is a card being treated as the whole story?** If yes, recommend moving details into visual artifacts.
- **Does this stakeholder need a different view?** If yes, suggest a role-specific visualization (UX sketch, risk map, test plan, roadmap).
- **Is the team using a tool as a replacement for a wall?** If yes, recommend live collaboration and simple digital whiteboards instead.
- **Is information stuck in a tool icebox?** If yes, recommend making it visible as an information radiator or shared board.
- **Does the team need tracking or understanding?** Use tracking charts for progress and visual story maps for understanding.
- **Are conversations being documented?** If not, advise photographing and archiving the visuals as vacation photos.

---

## Quality criteria

A strong story visualization recommendation should ensure the team is:

- **shared:** everyone can see and contribute to the same model,
- **focused:** visuals support the current type of conversation,
- **lightweight:** artifacts are easy to create, change, and discard,
- **visible:** important information radiates to the team,
- **preserved:** key decisions are captured for later recall,
- **tool-appropriate:** the right tool is used for collaboration versus tracking.
