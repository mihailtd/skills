# Rock Breaking

This skill helps AI describe the lifecycle of stories as a series of conversations that transform large opportunities into small, buildable work and then into evaluated product releases.

---

## When to use this skill

Use this skill when you need to:

- explain how stories travel from idea to product,
- help teams size stories differently for users, development, and business,
- break large stories into smaller stories through conversation,
- avoid breaking every big story into tiny backlog items too early,
- use epics and themes without weaponizing them,
- keep discovery, delivery, evaluation, and release connected.

---

## Outcome

Produce guidance that:

- frames stories as rocks that can be broken down repeatedly,
- emphasizes that the right size depends on the conversation,
- warns against breaking all big stories into tiny backlog clutter too soon,
- shows how opportunity discussions should be a go/forward or trash decision,
- encourages discovery to find a minimum viable solution,
- promotes frequent story workshops and ad hoc conversations during delivery,
- insists on user and stakeholder evaluation before release,
- reminds teams that product life begins after delivery.

---

## Instructions for the AI

1. **Explain the different views of story size**
   - Describe a user-sized story as something that fulfills a need.
   - Describe a development-sized story as something that can be built and tested in a few days.
   - Describe a business-sized story as something that achieves a meaningful business outcome.
   - Stress that there is no single “right” size; the right size depends on the conversation.

2. **Use the asteroid and rock-breaking metaphors**
   - Explain that big stories break into smaller stories, and those smaller stories break again.
   - Use the Asteroids metaphor: shooting all the big rocks at once fills the backlog with tiny pieces flying every which way.
   - Warn that breaking all big stories too early creates backlog clutter, loss of big-picture context, and needless complexity.
   - Reinforce that story splitting should happen progressively and just in time, with different conversations serving different purposes.
   - Explain that small stories can be reassembled into larger cards when backlog management needs a bigger handle.

3. **Warn against using terms as weapons**
   - Acknowledge that terms like epic and theme are useful but can be misused.
   - Encourage teams to avoid saying “that’s an epic” as a way to dismiss someone’s request.
   - Recommend using epics and themes as organizing constructs while keeping the focus on shared storytelling.

4. **Start with opportunity conversations**
   - Recommend capturing ideas as opportunities and sorting them into an opportunity backlog.
   - Describe the first conversation as a go-forward or trash decision to filter out bad ideas early.
   - Explain that go-forward means spending time on discovery, not necessarily building right away.

5. **Discover a minimum viable solution**
   - Recommend discovery work that names users, current alternatives, desired outcomes, and solution ideas.
   - Encourage validating assumptions with customers, prototypes, and technical spikes.
   - Advise teams to trim anything that is not part of the smallest valuable solution.

6. **Use story workshops during delivery**
   - Recommend deep-dive discussions with developers, testers, and other builders before the next sprint.
   - Call these “last best conversations” because they define the acceptance criteria for the next pieces of work.
   - Encourage these workshops to happen frequently, not just once during planning.

7. **Keep talking as you build**
   - Explain that development will expose new questions, and teams should have frequent ad hoc conversations.
   - Advise developers to seek answers quickly rather than blaming requirements.
   - Encourage product people and designers to review progress early and give feedback.

8. **Evaluate each piece with customers and stakeholders**
   - Recommend pausing to inspect working software from both quality and value perspectives.
   - Advise testing meaningful chunks with users, not just show-and-tell demos.
   - Encourage stakeholder reviews that focus on progress toward the minimum viable solution.

9. **Release and continue learning**
   - Emphasize that shipping is not the end; it is the start of the product lifecycle.
   - Recommend using metrics and customer conversations to learn whether target outcomes were met.
   - Advise feeding post-release learning back into the opportunity backlog.

---

## Decision points and guidance

- **Is the story too large from a development perspective?** If yes, break it down through conversation.
- **Is someone using “epic” as a reprimand?** If yes, steer the discussion back to collaboration and shared understanding.
- **Should this opportunity be discarded early?** If yes, celebrate trashing mediocre work and freeing capacity.
- **Is discovery still uncovering assumptions?** If yes, use prototypes and spikes to validate before delivery.
- **Are users and stakeholders seeing enough to learn?** If no, make the next slice more meaningful.
- **Is the backlog filling with tiny stories and losing the big picture?** If yes, bundle related small stories into larger cards or themes.
- **Has shipping become the finish line?** If yes, remind the team that product life begins after release.

---

## Quality criteria

A strong rock-breaking response should confirm that the team is:

- **conversational:** using dialogue to break and refine stories,
- **context-aware:** sizing stories for the current audience,
- **disciplined:** separating opportunity, discovery, delivery, evaluation, and release,
- **collaborative:** involving the right people in story workshops,
- **learning-focused:** evaluating work with users and feeding results back,
- **product-minded:** seeing shipped work as the start of the next cycle.
