# Story-Driven Collaboration

This skill helps AI explain how to use stories as a collaborative process rather than a requirements-writing activity. It emphasizes the 3 Cs, shared understanding, and visual storytelling using words and pictures.

---

## When to use this skill

Use this skill when you need to:

- help teams move from document-based requirements to story conversations,
- explain why stories are for use, not just for writing,
- teach the Kent Beck / Ron Jeffries 3 Cs: Card, Conversation, Confirmation,
- encourage story conversations that surface who, what, why, and how,
- recommend visual and digital artifacts that capture the conversation.

---

## Outcome

Produce guidance that:

- shifts teams from writing perfect stories to having rich story conversations,
- clarifies the real value of stories as shared understanding,
- shows how story cards fit into a product backlog and story map,
- helps teams use simple visual artifacts, whiteboards, and digital diagrams,
- explains how to capture and revisit conversations with vacation photos,
- warns against template zombies and over-reliance on story format alone.

---

## Instructions for the AI

1. **Explain the story mindset**
   - Emphasize that stories are meant to be used through conversation,
     not to be perfect documents.
   - Explain that the real problem with requirements is not only misunderstanding,
     but often building the wrong thing because the context and why were missing.
   - Reinforce that the best solutions emerge when problem owners and builders collaborate.

2. **Teach the 3 Cs**
   - Card: write a short story or task card to capture a candidate idea.
   - Conversation: use the card to start a rich discussion about users and outcomes.
   - Confirmation: agree on how the work will be validated and what it means to be done.
   - Recommend the 3 Cs as a simple process for keeping stories grounded and useful.

3. **Focus on the right conversation topics**
   - Ask teams to talk about specific users, not generic users.
   - Ask them to discuss what users need to do and why they care.
   - Ask them to probe assumptions, questions, pain points, and alternatives.
   - Explain that how and how long also matter, because cost and risk are real.

4. **Encourage words and pictures**
   - Recommend combining cards with personas, workflow sketches, and UI ideas.
   - Explain that visual artifacts help people point, correct, and remember.
   - Advise teams to capture what was decided, who participated, and what open questions remain.
   - Suggest using whiteboards, flipchart paper, or diagram tools to create shared visual context.

5. **Include digital visualizations**
   - Recommend digitizing map snapshots, story boards, and artifact photos.
   - Suggest tools like diagram editors, whiteboard apps, or simple chart tools to create digital versions.
   - Explain how digital visualizations can be used as reference, shared artifacts, and living documentation.
   - Advise keeping digital visuals simple: story flow diagrams, user-task flows, and annotated cards.

6. **Warn against template obsession**
   - Call out “template zombies” who let templates drive the work instead of the thinking.
   - Explain that the name of the story template is not the point; the conversation is.
   - Recommend using simple titles and a compact Who/What/Why/How format when cards need context.
   - Encourage teams to write only what helps start and enrich the conversation.

7. **Promote learning practices**
   - Recommend small-group story conversations before large backlog planning sessions.
   - Advise teams to use lightweight, frequent conversations rather than huge, infrequent meetings.
   - Suggest photographing boards or whiteboards as “vacation photos” to preserve the context.
   - Remind teams that story work is iterative, and the artifacts should evolve as understanding grows.

---

## Decision points and guidance

- **Is the team still focused on writing instead of talking?** If yes, refocus on conversation.
- **Does the story have a clear who, what, and why?** If not, ask those questions explicitly.
- **Are assumptions and open questions visible?** If not, surface them immediately.
- **Is the story being treated as a contract or as a conversation starter?** If the former, shift it.
- **Are visuals supporting the discussion?** If not, introduce sketches, workflows, or map snapshots.
- **Is the team relying on story format over meaning?** If yes, remind them that stories are about shared understanding.

---

## Quality criteria

A strong story-driven collaboration response should ensure the team is:

- **conversational:** stories spark dialogue, not monologue,
- **contextual:** user, purpose, and environment are explicit,
- **visual:** words are supported by simple diagrams and artifact snapshots,
- **evidence-based:** assumptions and questions are captured,
- **validatable:** acceptance criteria or confirmation points are agreed,
- **adaptive:** the story evolves as the team learns more.
