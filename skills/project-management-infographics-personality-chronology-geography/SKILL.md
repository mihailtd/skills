# Infographics for Who, When, and Where

This skill helps AI explain how to use infographics to communicate personality, history, timing, and geographic context across internal and external audiences.

## When to use this skill

Use this skill when you need to:

- humanize a company or brand with values, culture, or personality,
- explain a company’s history, product timeline, or project chronology,
- present location-based information such as maps, regional metrics, or distribution,
- choose an infographic format for brand storytelling, recruitment, or context-rich content,
- avoid jargon and make organization-focused visuals feel authentic and useful.

## Outcome

Produce guidance that:

- presents personality infographics as tools for brand humanization and trust-building,
- explains how visual resumes and personality visuals differ from formal corporate content,
- recommends timeline infographics for company history, product launches, industry milestones, and project schedules,
- shows how geographic visuals help audiences understand locations, regional performance, distribution, and scope,
- argues for simple, human-centered visuals that make identity, chronology, and place feel real.

## Instructions for the AI

1. **Explain personality and brand humanization**
   - Describe how infographics can express values, style, personality, and culture without slick marketing clichés.
   - Explain that good brand visuals help audiences connect emotionally while conveying what the organization stands for.
   - Mention examples like values infographics, cartoons, or visual resumes that make an organization feel more human.

2. **Teach when to use timelines and chronology**
   - Recommend timeline infographics for business history, product/service evolution, industry context, and project schedules.
   - Explain that timelines make it easier to absorb a sequence of events and see how the organization has developed over time.
   - Advise selecting the most relevant milestones and avoiding clutter by focusing on the story the audience needs.

3. **Describe geographic communication**
   - Explain that maps and geographics are useful for visualizing locations, regional scope, market presence, and distribution.
   - Recommend using geographic visuals for company location data, regional metrics, research findings, and delivery or sales coverage.
   - Emphasize that maps should highlight meaningful patterns rather than just display points on a page.

4. **Warn about excess and authenticity**
   - Warn that overusing jargon or generic buzzwords undermines humanization and makes infographics forgettable.
   - Emphasize that personality visuals should feel specific to the brand and avoid substitutable marketing language.
   - Advise that visual resumes and personal branding graphics are tools, not substitutes for strong content and honest positioning.

5. **Advise on practical execution**
   - Recommend prioritizing clarity, authenticity, and readability over visual flair.
   - Suggest repurposing personality infographics across web, training, and presentation contexts.
   - Advise keeping timelines and maps focused on the audience’s needs, with clear legends, dates, and geography labels.

## Decision points and guidance

- **If the goal is brand connection:** choose personality or values visuals rather than product-focused charts.
- **If the audience needs history or sequence:** choose a timeline or chronology infographic.
- **If the message is location-based:** choose maps, regional overlays, or distribution visualizations.
- **If the content is too generic:** replace buzzwords with concrete examples and audience-centered language.
- **If the visual is meant for recruitment or personal branding:** treat infographic resumes as a complement to a strong narrative, not a gimmick.
- **If your infographic tries to show too much:** break it into separate visuals for personality, time, and place.

## Quality criteria

A strong response should be:

- **personal:** the visual feels like it belongs to a specific organization or individual,
- **narrative-driven:** the timeline or geography tells a clear story,
- **audience-focused:** it answers a real information need,
- **authentic:** it avoids generic marketing language and meaningless buzzwords,
- **clear:** the structure, labels, and progression are easy to follow,
- **practical:** it is useful for website content, onboarding, presentations, or stakeholder communication.
