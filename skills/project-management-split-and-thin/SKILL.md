# Split and Thin

This skill helps AI explain how to turn large or complex stories into smaller, more manageable pieces by splitting work and removing unnecessary weight.

---

## When to use this skill

Use this skill when you need to:

- make a large story small enough to build and test quickly,
- avoid the big-plan trap of splitting work by technical layers,
- find the smallest “cupcake-sized” increments that still deliver value,
- use good-better-best thinking to separate basics from extras,
- keep iterative refinement and learning moving forward.

---

## Outcome

Produce guidance that:

- describes why smaller stories are easier to build, test, and learn from,
- explains that splitting is not a loss of ambition but a way to get feedback sooner,
- shows how to make a story good enough, better, and best,
- encourages breaking big stories into complete slices rather than large layers,
- reminds teams to inspect work and refine acceptance during the build.

---

## Instructions for the AI

1. **Explain the benefit of smaller stories**
   - Explain that smaller stories make progress visible and reduce risk.
   - Describe the “cupcake” metaphor: build small working pieces rather than a giant cake.
   - Stress that smaller slices let the team learn sooner and adjust faster.

2. **Use splitting and thinning**
   - Recommend breaking stories into smaller stories when they are larger than a few days of work.
   - Explain that thinning means removing extras and focusing on the smallest viable version.
   - Advise preserving user intent while making each story easier to complete.

3. **Introduce Good-Better-Best**
   - Explain Good as the smallest thing that will work, Better as useful enhancements, and Best as delightful options.
   - Recommend treating Good items as the first stories to build.
   - Advise keeping Better and Best ideas visible for later refinement or release.

4. **Tie refinement to acceptance**
   - Suggest using acceptance criteria to reveal the parts that can be split into separate stories.
   - Explain that the acceptance conversation often points to smaller, testable chunks.
   - Recommend using those chunks as a roadmap for incremental delivery.

5. **Avoid layer-based breakdowns**
   - Warn against splitting work into separate backend, frontend, and data phases.
   - Encourage end-to-end slices that still deliver user value.
   - Explain that layer-based work delays feedback and hides integration risk.

---

## Decision points and guidance

- **Is the story too big to build and test in a few days?** If yes, split or thin it.
- **Are team members struggling to agree on effort?** If yes, break the story into smaller pieces.
- **Is the story a mix of must-haves and nice-to-haves?** If yes, classify them as Good, Better, and Best.
- **Does the story only make sense as a large chunk?** If yes, look for the smallest meaningful user outcome to deliver first.
- **Is the team using layer-based plans?** If yes, steer them toward cross-functional slices.

---

## Quality criteria

A strong split-and-thin response should ensure the team is:

- **incremental:** choosing smaller slices that can be built and reviewed,
- **value-focused:** keeping user and business value central,
- **disciplined:** removing unnecessary complexity,
- **pragmatic:** preserving learnable outcomes,
- **adaptive:** willing to refine stories as new information emerges,
- **predictable:** able to estimate smaller stories more reliably.

---

## Example prompts

- "How can we break this big story down into smaller stories?"
- "What’s the Good-Better-Best way to split a feature?"
- "Should we build the whole thing now or split it into cupcakes?"
