# Visualizing How Things Work and Are Connected

This skill helps AI explain how to use infographics for business process, organizational structure, hierarchical concepts, relationship maps, systems thinking, and business models.

## When to use this skill

Use this skill when you need to:

- explain a business process or workflow to customers, employees, or partners,
- show how products, services, or ideas are connected,
- communicate organizational hierarchy, pricing tiers, or decision-making structure,
- use infographics for training, onboarding, or internal process alignment,
- help stakeholders grasp systems thinking or business models visually,
- avoid jargon and center infographics on the audience’s information needs.

## Outcome

Produce guidance that:

- positions process infographics as explanations of how a business operates,
- identifies the primary process categories that organizations communicate visually,
- describes how hierarchy and relationship infographics help audiences navigate structure and connections,
- explains when visual metaphors, process maps, or service models are the right choice,
- warns against overloading infographics with jargon, irrelevant detail, or too much scope.

## Instructions for the AI

1. **Define process and structure infographics**
   - Explain that process visuals help people understand how things work, from strategy and governance to operations, marketing, customer care, and administration.
   - Describe how a process infographic makes routines and decision flows easier to follow than dense text.
   - Recommend using process visuals for both internal and external audiences when the goal is understanding, not persuasion alone.

2. **Frame business categories clearly**
   - Recommend using infographics for management, operations, marketing, customer care, and administration activities.
   - Explain that each category can be made tangible with appropriate metaphors and process steps.
   - Highlight that processes should be shown from the audience’s viewpoint, not the business’s internal jargon.

3. **Explain the jargon dilemma**
   - Warn that overly complex business language makes infographics less useful.
   - Emphasize plain language, audience-focused labels, and clear outcomes.
   - Recommend replacing corporate buzzwords with customer-centric terms that answer “What does this mean for me?”

4. **Show internal and external uses**
   - Describe internal infographics for onboarding, training, and embedding actions through simpler process visuals.
   - Describe external infographics for pre- and post-purchase journeys, policy explanations, and service expectations.
   - Recommend making internal visuals actionable by showing next steps and decision points.

5. **Describe hierarchy and relationship visualizations**
   - Explain that hierarchies represent people, products, pricing, or conceptual layers.
   - Recommend organizational charts, pricing grids, and inverted hierarchies for structure and accountability.
   - Explain that relationship maps help audiences see connections among people, processes, systems, and ideas.

6. **Support systems thinking and business models**
   - Explain that systems thinking visuals compress large systems into accessible diagrams.
   - Recommend using business model infographics to show how value is created, delivered, and captured.
   - Advise using these visuals as conversation starters rather than final deliverables when models are complex.

7. **Advise on effective infographic design**
   - Recommend simple, focused visuals that highlight a small number of important ideas.
   - Warn against scope creep and trying to show every detail in one graphic.
   - Encourage clear labels, consistent structure, and a narrative flow that leads the viewer through the information.

## Decision points and guidance

- **If the audience needs to understand a workflow:** choose a process map or journey diagram.
- **If the goal is to explain structure:** choose hierarchies, org charts, or pricing tier visuals.
- **If the goal is to surface connections:** choose relationship maps, ecosystems, or network diagrams.
- **If the message is a business model:** choose a high-level model or value-creation infographic and keep it simple.
- **If the audience is unfamiliar with jargon:** replace specialized terms with everyday language.
- **If the infographic feels too dense:** split it into multiple visuals or use a stepped story format.

## Quality criteria

A strong response should be:

- **audience-centered:** designed around what the viewer needs to understand,
- **clear:** showing process, structure, or relationships without confusion,
- **simple:** avoiding unnecessary jargon and excessive detail,
- **actionable:** helping people know what to do or where to go next,
- **connective:** making relationships and systems visible,
- **scalable:** useful as a primer or a conversation starter, not the only source of truth.

## Example prompts

- "How do we show our customer onboarding process in a way that non-technical people can follow?"
- "What kind of infographic should we use to explain our pricing tiers and service levels?"
- "How can we visualize the relationships between our products, partners, and customers?"
- "When should we use a business-model infographic versus an organizational chart?"
