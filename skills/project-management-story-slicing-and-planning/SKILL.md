# Story Slicing and Planning

This skill helps AI explain how story conversations turn into a work plan, why big stories need to be sliced into smaller buildable pieces, and how to keep planning focused on learning and progress.

---

## When to use this skill

Use this skill when you need to:

- move from story conversations to a delivery plan,
- help teams understand the difference between telling the story and writing the work plan,
- break large stories into smaller, evaluable slices,
- avoid layer-based big plans that delay learning,
- avoid breaking every story into tiny backlog items too early,
- choose smaller increments that let teams see progress sooner.

---

## Outcome

Produce guidance that:

- describes story conversations as shared recipe discovery,
- explains that the story is about customer goals and context, not implementation tasks,
- helps teams create a plan of development tasks after the story conversation,
- encourages slicing large stories into small, testable increments,
- warns against breaking every big story into tiny backlog clutter before the right time,
- recommends using story maps and MVP thinking to keep the plan small and useful.

---

## Instructions for the AI

1. **Use the cake recipe metaphor**
   - Explain that story conversations are like ordering a cake: they define the who, what, why, and context.
   - Explain that the work plan is like the baker’s recipe and checklist: it contains the specific tasks needed to make the cake.
   - Emphasize that the buyer doesn’t need to tell the baker how to measure flour; they need to help the baker know the right cake to make.

2. **Distinguish story definition from planning**
   - Recommend keeping story conversations focused on the desired outcome, users, and context.
   - Recommend creating the plan separately, once the story is understood.
   - Warn against forcing the development plan into the story conversation itself.

3. **Break down big stories thoughtfully**
   - Recommend slicing large stories into small, workable pieces rather than big, functional work streams.
   - Explain that big stories do not mean big plans; they mean more careful slicing.
   - Use the cupcake metaphor: create smaller pieces that can be tasted and evaluated sooner.

4. **Split progressively and just in time**
   - Explain that story splitting should happen at the point when the team is ready to learn from the work.
   - Warn that breaking every big story into tiny pieces too early can create backlog clutter and obscure the product vision.
   - Recommend preserving larger cards until discovery and cycle planning clarify where the smallest meaningful splits belong.

5. **Avoid big-plan anti-patterns**
   - Call out the trap of splitting work by layers (UI, backend, data) instead of by small customer-focused increments.
   - Explain that layer-based plans delay feedback and make the team wait too long to learn.
   - Recommend cross-cutting, end-to-end slices that still feel like working parts of the solution.

6. **Keep learning as the goal**
   - Recommend building what you can learn from first, not only what you can build fastest.
   - Explain that each slice should expose assumptions and validate the story.
   - Encourage planning and writing follow-up stories for improvement after each learning cycle.

6. **Use story maps as slicing tools**
   - Recommend using story maps to visualize the big story and then slice it into prioritized increments.
   - Advise teams to place work above and below release lines, with target outcomes for each slice.
   - Ensure story map slices focus on delivery and learning, not just on completion of tasks.

---

## Decision points and guidance

- **Is the story describing a big solution?** If yes, break it into smaller evaluable pieces.
- **Does the team plan by layers or by customer value?** If by layers, recommend cross-functional slices.
- **Is the first slice big enough to learn from?** If not, make it smaller and more focused.
- **Is the team treating the story as the work plan?** If yes, separate the story conversation from task planning.
- **Are the slices delivering progress sooner?** If not, adjust to smaller increments that still add value.
- **Are we breaking stories down too early and creating needless backlog clutter?** If yes, keep the story larger until the next appropriate discussion.

---

## Quality criteria

A good story-slicing and planning response should ensure the plan is:

- **outcome-focused:** slices support meaningful user goals,
- **small enough:** each slice is evaluable and can be reviewed soon,
- **separate:** story definition and development planning are distinct,
- **learnable:** each slice converts assumptions into validated learning,
- **visible:** progress is clear and the team can measure momentum.
