# Start with Opportunities

This skill helps AI explain how to begin product work with opportunity conversations, how to decide which ideas deserve deeper discovery, and how to use canvases and story maps to organize that thinking.

---

## When to use this skill

Use this skill when you need to:

- evaluate early product ideas before committing to build,
- keep idea discussions focused on who, what, why, and size,
- make clear go/forward or trash decisions,
- organize opportunities into an opportunity backlog,
- avoid breaking every opportunity into tiny backlog items before discovery,
- use a canvas to structure discovery discussions,
- connect opportunity thinking to story mapping and product context.

---

## Outcome

Produce guidance that:

- treats early ideas as opportunities, not automatic requirements,
- encourages the team to discuss users, problems, solution ideas, business benefit, and size,
- makes no-go decisions visible and celebrated,
- recommends deeper discovery only for promising opportunities,
- warns against decomposing every opportunity into tiny backlog items too early,
- uses a canvas format to gather and relate opportunity information,
- keeps story maps for product context and opportunity spotting.

---

## Instructions for the AI

1. **Frame opportunities as the first story conversation**
   - Explain that an opportunity is the first, biggest rock in a story flow.
   - Recommend discussing who it is for, what problems it solves, what you imagine building, why it matters, and how big it feels.
   - Emphasize that this early conversation is about deciding whether to proceed, not about building yet.

2. **Encourage clear go/forward or trash decisions**
   - Explain that “go” means move the opportunity into deeper discovery, not commit to build.
   - Explain that “no-go” is a valid and valuable result that frees capacity for better opportunities.
   - Recommend involving idea champions in the decision so they understand and support the outcome.

3. **Handle uncertainty with learning actions**
   - Tell teams that if they can’t decide, they should list what they need to learn and then do that discovery work.
   - Suggest putting undecided opportunities back into the backlog when more information is needed.
   - Normalize deliberate procrastination when it’s a better choice than premature commitment.

4. **Use an Opportunity Canvas**
   - Recommend a canvas-driven approach to organize opportunity information in a single view.
   - Explain that a canvas makes it easier to see relationships between problems, users, metrics, business needs, adoption, and budget.
   - Advise teams to co-create the canvas with sticky notes, iterate it, and update it as they learn.

5. **Avoid early over-splitting**
   - Explain that the opportunity stage is for strategic evaluation, not for creating every tiny backlog item.
   - Recommend keeping opportunity cards larger until discovery and planning reveal the best places to split.
   - Advise teams to bundle related tiny stories back into bigger cards later if backlog clutter becomes a problem.

6. **Explain canvas structure and flow**
   - Suggest starting with problems or solutions and moving through users, current solutions, user value, metrics, adoption, business problem, business metrics, and budget.
   - Explain that the canvas is a discussion tool, not a form to complete perfectly.
   - Recommend filling what is known, recording assumptions, and proceeding rather than stalling.

7. **Use story maps for product context, not opportunity management**
   - Explain that story maps are useful for understanding current product journeys and spotting opportunity hot spots.
   - Recommend using a high-level “now” map of the existing product to place opportunities in context.
   - Advise teams to look for frequent, high-value activities and pain points that make better opportunity candidates.

7. **Help teams stay picky**
   - Encourage aggressive trimming of opportunities that are unlikely to deliver meaningful outcomes.
   - Explain that not every idea should become software, even if someone champions it.
   - Recommend working with business stakeholders to make those tough decisions.

---

## Decision points and guidance

- **Is this idea still just an idea?** If yes, treat it as an opportunity rather than a requirement.
- **Can the team make a go/no-go decision?** If yes, either move it to discovery or trash it.
- **Does the opportunity lack enough information?** If yes, list what you need to learn and get that information.
- **Is the opportunity aligned to business strategy and customer impact?** If no, consider trashing it.
- **Is the canvas helping reveal assumptions or gaps?** If yes, keep iterating it with the team.
- **Does the story map show opportunity hot spots?** If yes, use that context to prioritize what to investigate.

---

## Quality criteria

A strong opportunity response should ensure the team is:

- **deliberate:** not treating every idea as a buildable requirement,
- **contextual:** thinking about users, problems, value, and business benefit,
- **decisive:** making go/no-go choices and valuing no-go outcomes,
- **collaborative:** using canvases and shared sessions to build understanding,
- **strategic:** connecting opportunities back to product context and roadmap,
- **learning-focused:** surfacing assumptions and planning what to learn next.
