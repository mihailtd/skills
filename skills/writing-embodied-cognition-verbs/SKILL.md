# Embodied Cognition Verbs (Stimulate the Brain)

## Goal
Transform text by choosing verbs that biologically and physiologically stimulate the reader's brain. This editor applies the science of "embodied cognition" and "fictive motion" to trigger the reader's motor cortex, forcing them to subconsciously simulate the actions and pacing of the narrative. It hunts for dead metaphors and weak descriptors, replacing them with visceral, physical actions.

## Checklist for Writing Review
- [ ] **Modulate Verb Intensity**: Evaluate standard action verbs (e.g., "hit", "fall") and upgrade them to higher-intensity, specific variants (e.g., "smash", "plummet") to actively alter the reader's perception and memory of the event.
- [ ] **Apply "Fictive Motion" to Inanimate Objects**: Check descriptions of static settings or objects (trails, roads, cords). Replace static verbs ("is", "goes") with kinetic verbs that dictate tempo and mood (e.g., "meanders", "plunges", "whips").
- [ ] **Trigger Visceral Empathy**: Scan emotional scenes. Ensure the character's internal states are expressed through highly physical, sensorimotor verbs (e.g., "pant", "foam", "amble") so the reader feels the physiological echo.
- [ ] **Resurrect or Replace Dead Metaphors**: Identify clichéd action metaphors (e.g., "kicked off a campaign", "swallowed his pride"). Because the brain ignores overused metaphors, rewrite them into unusual, fresh physical metaphors that force the motor cortex to light up.

## Anti-patterns (What to Avoid)
- **Low-Impact, Generic Action**: Using verbs that convey facts without sensory weight.
  - *Bad*: "The cars hit each other." / "She breathed heavily."
- **Static Geography**: Describing environments as frozen, unmoving things.
  - *Bad*: "The road goes along the coast." / "The cord is on the wall."
- **Dead Metaphors**: Relying on corporate or standardized idioms that fail to trigger a sensory response.
  - *Bad*: "We kicked off the event." / "The company is crawling back." (If used so often it loses its shock value).

## Good Patterns (What to Do)
- **High-Fidelity Sensorimotor Verbs**: Unleashing words that carry their own sound effect or physical weight.
  - *Good*: "The cars smashed together." / "The car whizzed past." / "I foamed, I raved, I swore."
- **Tempo-Controlling Fictive Motion**: Using verbs that literally slow down or speed up the reader's brain.
  - *Good*: "The road meanders along the coast." (Slowing down) / "The road knifes through the mountains." (Speeding up)
- **Unconventional Physical Metaphors**: Inventing fresh action metaphors that force the reader to simulate the movement.
  - *Good*: "He ambled down the catwalk, bouncing and rolling a bit from side to side."

## Examples

### Example 1: Applying Fictive Motion for Tempo
**Original:** The hiking trail goes north from the valley and then is suddenly on the edge of the cliff.
**Edited:** The hiking trail meanders north from the valley and then plunges toward the edge of the cliff.

### Example 2: Upgrading Verb Intensity for Memory Impact
**Original:** When the two football players ran into each other, the crowd gasped.
**Edited:** When the two football players smashed into each other, the crowd gasped.

### Example 3: Injecting Visceral Empathy
**Original:** I was nervous and breathing hard as I stood on the high bridge. The man walked past me, looking very relaxed.
**Edited:** I panted as the abyss pressed in from the high bridge. The man strolled past me as if he were ambling down the street, his harness bouncing and rolling from side to side.
