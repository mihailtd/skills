# Story Mapping Basics

This skill helps AI explain the foundational story-mapping concepts that make the
practice easy and intuitive, even for people who have never done it before.

---

## When to use this skill

Use this skill when you need to:

- teach teams the basic building blocks of story maps,
- convert everyday behavior into user tasks and narrative flow,
- show how to organize sticky notes into a backbone of activities,
- explore alternative stories, variations, and exceptions,
- use map slices to identify the tasks needed for a specific outcome.

---

## Outcome

Produce guidance that:

- defines tasks as short verb phrases that describe what people do,
- explains narrative flow as left-to-right storytelling order,
- shows how activities form the backbone of the map,
- encourages teams to surface variations and alternate paths,
- supports using the map to slice the work for different goals.

---

## Instructions for the AI

1. **Explain tasks clearly**
   - Describe user tasks as short, action-oriented phrases.
   - Point out that tasks are the most natural building blocks for story maps.
   - Warn that the word task can mean different things in project plans or Agile,
     and clarify the story-mapping meaning.

2. **Introduce narrative flow**
   - Recommend arranging tasks left-to-right in the order they happen.
   - Explain that this order is the story’s flow and connects tasks with “and then.”
   - Advise teams to tell the story out loud by pointing at tasks from left to right.

3. **Describe activities and the backbone**
   - Recommend grouping related tasks under higher-level activities.
   - Explain that activities are a row of related tasks and form the map backbone.
   - Encourage naming activities in the language of users, not the team.

4. **Encourage variation and exception handling**
   - Advise teams to capture alternate stories, edge cases, and ideal outcomes.
   - Explain that variations can be added in columns and left-to-right flow preserved.
   - Emphasize that exploring alternatives reveals real user behavior.

5. **Use slices for outcomes**
   - Explain how to draw a slice across the map for a specific goal.
   - Recommend moving tasks above the slice that are needed to reach that outcome.
   - Advise leaving activities with no tasks to show omitted areas deliberately.

6. **Link basic mapping to practical use**
   - Show how a “now map” can be created from current behavior to learn how work happens.
   - Explain that the same concepts apply to future product maps.
   - Recommend starting simple and using the exercise to build shared understanding.

---

## Quality criteria

A strong story-mapping basics response should ensure that the guidance is:

- **accessible:** easy for non-experts to follow,
- **actionable:** ready to use with sticky notes or a whiteboard,
- **behavioral:** focused on what people do, not screens or features,
- **visual:** using left-to-right flow and activity grouping,
- **outcome-oriented:** using slices to clarify goals.

---

## Example prompts

- "Teach my team the basic story-mapping concepts using a simple example."
- "How do we turn what people do into tasks and activities on a story map?"
- "Explain how to use slices to identify the tasks needed for a specific goal."
