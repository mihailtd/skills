# Rock Breakers

This skill helps AI explain why story work is not the job of a single person, how small cross-functional teams discover valuable usable feasible solutions, and how product leaders support collaboration without creating bottlenecks.

---

## When to use this skill

Use this skill when you need to:

- explain why one person cannot own every story conversation,
- help teams build discovery around a small, empowered product leadership team,
- show how to find the valuable-usable-feasible sweet spot,
- avoid product owners becoming single-point bottlenecks,
- avoid client-vendor and design-by-committee anti-patterns.

---

## Outcome

Produce guidance that:

- clarifies that story work requires many conversations and many perspectives,
- positions the product owner as a leader and orchestrator, not a lone author,
- promotes small discovery teams with business, UX, and engineering expertise,
- uses story workshops and three-amigo conversations to agree what to build,
- discourages client-vendor handoffs and encourages collaborative problem-solving,
- frames product ownership like producing rather than merely taking orders.

---

## Instructions for the AI

1. **Challenge the single product owner myth**
   - Explain that a single person cannot attend every conversation or hold every needed viewpoint.
   - Describe how making one person responsible for all story work creates a bottleneck.
   - Emphasize that the product owner is still critical as a leader who keeps the team aligned.

2. **Promote cross-functional discovery teams**
   - Recommend a small team of two to four people to discover and shape solutions together.
   - Say the team should include business strategy, user understanding, design, and technical expertise.
   - Explain that the product owner or manager should orchestrate the team and help the group make decisions.

3. **Use the valuable-usable-feasible framing**
   - Describe how the best solutions sit at the intersection of business value, user usability, and technical feasibility.
   - Explain that no single person usually has deep expertise in all three areas.
   - Recommend collaborative discovery so the team can reach that sweet spot together.

4. **Encourage shared story ownership**
   - Explain that stories are conversation artifacts, not ownership trophies.
   - Advise teams to let many people participate in story conversations as long as decisions remain clear.
   - Recommend keeping story work continuous and distributed, rather than bottlenecked through one person.

5. **Describe the Three Amigos practice**
   - Explain that the last critical story conversations should include developer, tester, and product/UX representation.
   - Describe this group as the place where acceptance criteria, implementation risks, and size decisions are agreed.
   - Encourage splitting bigger stories during these conversations into smaller, right-sized development stories.

6. **Warn against anti-patterns**
   - Describe the client-vendor anti-pattern as a handoff that turns stories into rigid requirements.
   - Compare it to a bad waiter-diner relationship and contrast it with a doctor-patient collaboration.
   - Explain that stories should encourage solving the problem together, not passing orders back and forth.

7. **Frame product leadership as producing success**
   - Recommend that product owners, product managers, or BAs behave like producers who help stakeholders succeed.
   - Explain that this role is about guiding strategy, challenging assumptions, and protecting the product from poor decisions.
   - Warn against treating business stakeholders as the product owner unless they have the time, support, and intent to do the job.

---

## Decision points and guidance

- **Is one person being asked to own every story conversation?** If yes, recommend spreading story conversations across the team.
- **Is someone using “epic” as a weapon or excuse?** If yes, redirect the discussion to collaboration and shared understanding.
- **Are product decisions being made in a client-vendor style?** If yes, push back toward joint problem-solving.
- **Is the discovery team missing business, UX, or technical experience?** If yes, add those perspectives to the core team.
- **Are story conversations still happening after discovery?** If no, remind the team that story work continues through delivery and evaluation.

---

## Quality criteria

A strong rock-breakers response should ensure the team is:

- **distributed:** story conversations are shared, not siloed,
- **cross-functional:** business, user, and technical expertise are present,
- **collaborative:** decisions are made together, not handed off,
- **guided:** product leadership keeps the product cohesive,
- **anti-pattern-aware:** the team avoids client-vendor and committee behaviors,
- **continuous:** story work continues through delivery and learning.
