# Verb Versatility and Verbals Writing Review

This skill guides the review and improvement of writing by utilizing the versatility of verbs—specifically leveraging "verbals" (participles, gerunds, and verbal nouns) to inject action, economy, and dynamic energy into sentences.

## Core Principles

1.  **Utilize Participles (Verbs as Adjectives)**: Participles (usually ending in *-ing* or *-ed*) are verbs masquerading as adjectives. They describe nouns with the punch and vitality of their verb roots.
2.  **Employ Gerunds (Verbs as Actions-in-Noun-Form)**: Gerunds (ending in *-ing*) act as nouns but refer to general actions or processes. Because they retain verb characteristics, they can be modified by adverbs, keeping the sentence active.
3.  **Leverage Verbal Nouns (Action-Oriented Nouns)**: Verbal nouns are nouns formed from verbs (often ending in *-ing*, but acting fully as nouns taking articles like *a* or *the*). They carry the echo and vibration of their verb root, making abstract concepts feel more alive.

## Patterns & Anti-Patterns

### Anti-Pattern 1: Generic or Weak Adjectives
Instead of using bland adjectives to describe a noun, use a participle that conveys continuous or completed action.
*   **Weak:** It was a very loud noon. / She had a stomachache.
*   **Strong (Participle):** "Roaring noon." / Her kindness eased my "gnawing grievance."

### Anti-Pattern 2: Static Nouns for Actions
Instead of using a purely static noun, use a gerund modified by an adverb to emphasize the action's ongoing nature.
*   **Weak:** Her messy egg destruction was a problem.
*   **Strong (Gerund):** You can't make an omelet without "messily breaking" some eggs.

### Anti-Pattern 3: Flat Abstract Nouns
Replace flat abstract nouns with verbal nouns to give the concept movement and history.
*   **Weak:** The ghosts were a problem at the house. / The end of the evening.
*   **Strong (Verbal Noun):** A "haunting" / "their green going".

## Writing Review Checklist

When reviewing a piece of text, execute the following steps:

- [ ] **Scan for Weak Adjectives:** Highlight basic adjectives (e.g., *loud, bad, scary, hot*). Could a participle (*roaring, gnawing, purifying, baking*) provide a more specific, action-oriented description?
- [ ] **Check for Lost Action in Nouns:** Look for descriptions of processes. Could a standard noun be replaced by a Gerund to emphasize the action? (Remember: Gerunds take adverbs).
- [ ] **Look for "The [Noun]":** When you see articles modifying abstract concepts, consider if a Verbal Noun (*the baking, a haunting*) would add a deeper sense of motion or legacy to the concept.
- [ ] **Evaluate Imagery Economy:** Are you using a whole clause to describe a state that a single participle could handle? (e.g., "The skin that had been bitten by insects" -> "Her stung skin").
