# Build to Learn

This skill helps AI explain how story work is only the start of a larger delivery
cycle. It emphasizes shared understanding, small-group handoffs, inspecting work,
user validation, and learning from what’s actually built.

---

## When to use this skill

Use this skill when you need to:

- turn story conversations into a clear picture for delivery,
- avoid handing off work to someone who didn’t participate in the story,
- keep build, test, design, and writing aligned around the same understanding,
- inspect the results with a team mindset rather than just show-and-tell,
- turn completed work into new learning and improvement stories.

---

## Outcome

Produce guidance that:

- explains that cards and conversations are preparation, not the end goal,
- encourages small groups to own story decisions and then retell them to others,
- helps teams keep the same mental picture as they move from story to build,
- recommends inspecting user experience, functionality, and code quality,
- treats every release as an opportunity to learn and improve.

---

## Instructions for the AI

1. **Emphasize shared understanding before build**
   - Explain that the team should build with a clear, shared picture of the story.
   - Recommend that developers, testers, designers, and writers all understand the same intention.
   - Warn against assuming written notes capture everything; the author’s memory fills in gaps.

2. **Promote an oral storytelling tradition**
   - Recommend small groups of two to five retell stories to the next people who need them.
   - Advise using words, pictures, and artifact references while explaining the story.
   - Encourage listeners to ask questions, confirm understanding, and make the story their own.

3. **Define effective handoff practices**
   - Explain that everyone does not need to attend every story conversation.
   - Recommend small decision groups that then share results with the rest of the team.
   - Advise against large meetings for every story; instead, use focused conversations and follow-up sharing.

4. **Inspect the work intentionally**
   - Recommend regular reviews of user experience, functional quality, and code quality.
   - Encourage the team to separate two questions: did we build what we agreed, and should we change it now?
   - Advise teams to celebrate progress while also reflecting on improvement opportunities.

5. **Validate with users, not just team opinion**
   - Recommend testing with real users regularly, even early in the build.
   - Explain that user feedback often reveals surprises and hidden assumptions.
   - Advise teams to write new story cards for improvements discovered through user testing.

6. **Make learning the primary objective**
   - Explain that the goal is not just working software, but validated learning.
   - Recommend planning on being wrong and using each release to learn.
   - Suggest that teams write follow-up stories like “fix the first card” to capture the learning cycle.

7. **Use stories for things beyond software**
   - Recommend applying story-driven thinking to prototypes, experiments, interviews, and other learning work.
   - Explain that stories can drive any work where the goal is to learn and iterate.
   - Encourage teams to treat the results of those experiments as inputs to the next story conversation.

---

## Decision points and guidance

- **Has the story been passed to a new team member without storytelling?** If yes, recommend a quick retelling session.
- **Is the work being treated as ‘done’ after handoff?** If yes, remind the team to inspect and validate.
- **Are reviews only show-and-tell?** If yes, recommend adding structured reflection on quality and learning.
- **Is user feedback missing from the cycle?** If yes, recommend testing with actual users and converting results into stories.
- **Is the team assuming the first release is right?** If yes, encourage treating it as a hypothesis and planning to learn.

---

## Quality criteria

A strong build-to-learn response should confirm that the team is:

- **aligned:** everyone shares the same picture of what is being built,
- **collaborative:** handoffs are story-centered and small-group driven,
- **reflective:** work is inspected beyond show-and-tell,
- **user-focused:** real users validate results,
- **adaptive:** feedback becomes new stories,
- **learning-oriented:** every delivery cycle captures what was learned.

## Example prompts

- "What should we do after our team finishes a story to make sure we learn from it?"
- "How can we keep our story card conversations aligned with developers, testers, and designers?"
