# Processing Your Infographic Ideas

This skill helps AI explain how to capture raw observations, overcome the “I’m not an artist” mindset, document visual ideas, and synthesize information into infographic-ready concepts.

## When to use this skill

Use this skill when you need to:

- turn observations, conversations, and inspiration into infographic ideas,
- build habits for recording ideas before they are forgotten,
- encourage non-designers to think visually and create simple communication sketches,
- recommend tools and methods for documenting infographic thoughts,
- synthesize information using the five W’s and one H before moving into design.

## Outcome

Produce guidance that:

- frames infographic creation as communication, not fine art,
- encourages people to capture ideas quickly and often,
- shows how to document ideas with paper, digital tools, and lightweight systems,
- explains how to process and filter information before design,
- introduces the 5W1H questions as a synthesis framework.

## Instructions for the AI

1. **Explain the mindset shift**
   - Emphasize that infographics are business communication tools, not art projects.
   - Reinforce that anyone can doodle and capture ideas, even without formal design training.
   - Quote the principle that visual thinking is about conveying meaning, not creating gallery-worthy work.

2. **Teach idea capture habits**
   - Recommend recording thoughts as soon as they appear, because ideas are fleeting.
   - Describe the importance of keeping accessible tools handy, such as notebooks, scrap paper, bookmarks, and mobile apps.
   - Explain that capturing early drafts increases the chance that an idea will mature into a useful infographic.

3. **Recommend documentation methods**
   - Outline paper-based capture: notebooks, business-card scraps, Post-it notes, and bedside notepads.
   - Describe digital capture: bookmarking services, note apps, mobile sketchpads, voice memos, and photos.
   - Remind users that the method should fit their workflow and be simple enough to use regularly.

4. **Address the “I’m not an artist” barrier**
   - Normalize the fear of not being creative and then reframe it as a communication challenge.
   - Explain that the value of an infographic comes from the idea and the message, not from polished visuals.
   - Encourage experimentation, doodling, and iterating without judgment.

5. **Introduce infosynthesis**
   - Explain that the most important work happens between observation and design.
   - Describe infosynthesis as gathering, connecting, and shaping information into a visual framework.
   - Emphasize that this stage helps ensure the final infographic is relevant, clear, and usable.

6. **Use the 5W1H framework**
   - Explain who the audience is, what the key message should be, when the content is relevant and needs publishing, where the data comes from and the graphic will appear, why the information matters, and how easy it is to understand.
   - Encourage using these questions to filter and refine raw ideas.
   - Advise breaking complex topics into multiple visuals rather than forcing everything into one graphic.

## Decision points and guidance

- **If an idea feels too vague:** recommend recording it anyway, then revisit it later with more context.
- **If someone says “I’m not an artist”:** remind them that infographics are tools for communication and that simple visuals are enough.
- **If the idea is drowning in detail:** recommend filtering it through the 5W1H questions and choosing one strong message.
- **If the audience or channel is unclear:** recommend defining who will use the infographic and where it will be published before designing.
- **If the data source is questionable:** recommend validating sources and discarding unreliable inputs.
- **If the idea has multiple important points:** suggest creating a series of related infographics instead of a single overstuffed one.

## Quality criteria

A strong response should be:

- **practical:** it offers concrete ways to capture and document ideas,
- **encouraging:** it removes the intimidation of DIY infographic creation,
- **systematic:** it shows how to synthesize information before design,
- **audience-centered:** it keeps the intended viewer and channel in mind,
- **focused:** it helps turn raw observations into one clear idea,
- **iterative:** it treats early sketches as seeds, not final products.
