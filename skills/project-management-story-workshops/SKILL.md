# Story Workshops

This skill helps AI explain how to run story workshops as productive, story-focused conversations that refine rough backlog items into buildable, testable work.

---

## When to use this skill

Use this skill when you need to:

- turn backlog stories into detailed, shared understanding,
- stop treating planning as a long, painful meeting,
- include only the right people to keep conversations productive,
- make decisions about what to build, how it should work, and how to confirm it,
- discover that stories need to be split or thinned before development.

---

## Outcome

Produce guidance that:

- describes a story workshop as a focused, collaborative product conversation,
- recommends a small group of people who understand users, design, feasibility, and quality,
- explains that the goal is to refine rough stories into polished, build-ready nuggets,
- encourages the use of whiteboards, sketches, examples, and visible artifacts,
- instructs teams to document decisions, acceptance criteria, and break down stories as needed.

---

## Instructions for the AI

1. **Set the purpose clearly**
   - Explain that story workshops are about answering “exactly what will we build?”
   - Emphasize that they are not status updates or long review meetings.
   - Describe them as the mechanism that turns rough backlog items into polished work.

2. **Keep the group small**
   - Recommend three to five people to keep the conversation like a dinner conversation.
   - Include someone who understands users and the UI, one or two developers, and a tester.
   - Explain that one person can wear multiple hats, but missing concerns should be addressed.

3. **Use visuals and examples**
   - Encourage drawing sketches, workflows, data examples, and acceptance scenarios.
   - Recommend using concrete examples of user actions, data input, and expected outcomes.
   - Advise taking photos of whiteboards and transcribing important notes afterward.

4. **Discuss feasibility and size**
   - Explain that this is the point where the team can judge whether the story is too big.
   - Recommend breaking bigger stories into smaller pieces that can be built, tested, and demonstrated.
   - Ask the workshop to estimate the likely effort and identify technical or UX risks.

5. **Agree on confirmation**
   - Recommend capturing acceptance criteria and demo scenarios as the output of the workshop.
   - Explain that these criteria help the team know when the story is done.
   - Advise using them as the basis for later review and testing.

6. **Stay collaborative, not directive**
   - Warn against one person describing requirements while everyone else listens.
   - Encourage dialogue that considers user intent, implementation options, and quality concerns.
   - Explain that productive workshops are full of questions, options, and shared decisions.

---

## Decision points and guidance

- **Is the story still rough and incomplete?** If yes, workshop it before development.
- **Is the conversation too large or unfocused?** If yes, reduce participants or split the discussion.
- **Are acceptance criteria missing?** If yes, capture them before the story leaves the workshop.
- **Does the story feel too big for a cycle?** If yes, split it or thin it.
- **Is the workshop dominated by one perspective?** If yes, invite missing expertise or pause to regroup.

---

## Quality criteria

A strong story workshop response should ensure the team is:

- **focused:** the work is about a story, not the whole backlog,
- **small:** participants are productive and engaged,
- **visual:** ideas are sketched and examples are shared,
- **decisive:** decisions are made about what to build,
- **confirming:** acceptance and demo criteria are clear,
- **iterative:** stories are split or thinned when needed.
