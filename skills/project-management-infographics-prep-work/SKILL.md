# Infographic Prep Work

This skill helps AI explain how to prepare for infographic creation by defining purpose, understanding audiences, setting objectives, and building the observation habits needed to gather meaningful information.

## When to use this skill

Use this skill when you need to:

- plan infographic content strategically rather than making visuals on the fly,
- define the purpose and business outcomes for infographic communication,
- analyze audience information needs for customers, prospects, employees, and partners,
- set realistic infographic objectives and capacity-aware publishing goals,
- align infographics with your brand, content marketing, and communication strategy,
- develop observation and active listening habits that make infographic ideas more relevant.

## Outcome

Produce guidance that:

- positions infographics as a communication medium with an explicit purpose,
- helps teams identify the right audience for each visual,
- encourages objectives that are measurable, attainable, and aligned with business, brand, and marketing needs,
- emphasizes the difference between useful infographics and “rudderless” visuals,
- promotes observation and listening as core skills for infographic creation.

## Instructions for the AI

1. **Define infographic purpose**
   - Explain that every infographic needs a clear reason for existing.
   - Warn against design work that is only decorative or does not serve an audience need.
   - Recommend asking: Who is this for? What question does it answer? What action should it support?

2. **Teach audience analysis**
   - Explain the primary audience groups: customers, prospects, employees, job candidates, partners, and media.
   - Describe typical information needs for each group and the infographic ideas that serve them.
   - Emphasize that different audiences require different formats, detail levels, and language.

3. **Explain objective-setting**
   - Recommend setting objectives that reflect business outcomes, not just visual output.
   - Describe sample objectives for thought leadership, awareness, traffic, engagement, communication, and entertainment.
   - Explain that objectives can be informal but should anchor the infographic to a purpose and a broader communication strategy.

4. **Introduce SMART objectives**
   - Define Specific, Measurable, Attainable, Relevant, and Time-based goals.
   - Show how to convert a vague infographic goal into a SMART objective.
   - Explain that SMART goals make it easier to evaluate whether an infographic is working.

5. **Cover infographic capacity**
   - Explain that publishing frequency and quality are constrained by labor, budget, and design skill.
   - Advise teams to match their goals to their available capacity.
   - Recommend prioritizing high-value visuals over trying to produce too many infographics.

6. **Teach the art of observation**
   - Explain that good infographics start with understanding, not just visual execution.
   - Recommend cultivating curiosity, asking questions, and noticing unusual or meaningful patterns.
   - Link observation to audience listening and information gathering.

7. **Describe active listening methods**
   - Recommend using existing organizational knowledge: FAQs, customer service logs, and employee feedback.
   - Suggest talking to frontline teams, suppliers, HR, and support staff to uncover audience questions.
   - Advise using social listening and keyword tracking to discover audience conversations and information gaps.

## Decision points and guidance

- **If an infographic does not have a clear audience:** recommend pausing and defining who should benefit from it.
- **If the objective is vague:** recommend framing it in terms of business outcomes, not just shareability.
- **If capacity is limited:** recommend fewer, higher-value infographics rather than more mediocre visuals.
- **If the information is already known internally:** recommend validating it with actual audience questions or external data.
- **If the content feels generic:** recommend refining the message so it answers a specific audience need.
- **If the team wants to create a visual quickly:** recommend doing prep work first to avoid wasted effort.

## Quality criteria

A strong infographic prep-work response should be:

- **purposeful:** it starts with why the infographic exists,
- **audience-aware:** it identifies who needs the information and why,
- **objective-driven:** it suggests measurable or meaningful goals,
- **capacity-conscious:** it matches goals to realistic resources,
- **observant:** it treats listening and curiosity as part of the creative process,
- **strategic:** it helps the team create visuals that are useful, not just attractive.
