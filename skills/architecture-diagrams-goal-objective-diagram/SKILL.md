# Goal/Objective Diagram — Strategy Alignment Matrix

A Goal/Objective Diagram is a strategic architecture deliverable that maps
high-level goals to specific, measurable objectives. It helps architects show
how broad strategic intent is translated into actionable targets and how progress
can be measured.

This skill is used when an organization needs a clear, stakeholder-friendly
view of strategy, accountability, and execution readiness.

---

## When to use this skill

Use this skill when you need to:

- Translate strategic goals into measurable objectives.
- Visualize the relationship between goals and objectives.
- Identify objectives that lack goal alignment or goals that lack supporting
  objectives.
- Communicate strategy clearly to executives and stakeholders.
- Provide the basis for performance measurement and roadmap planning.

---

## Outcome

Produce a Goal/Objective Diagram or Matrix that shows:

- **Goals**: the organization’s high-level desired outcomes.
- **Objectives**: the specific, measurable targets that enable the goals.
- **Goal-objective relationships**: the explicit links showing which objectives
  contribute to which goals.
- **Strategic clarity**: whether objectives are SMART and whether goals are
  fully supported.

The result should make it easy to answer which objectives support which goals
and whether the strategy is actionable.

---

## Step-by-step workflow

1. **Collect strategic elements**
   - Review existing strategy documents, annual plans, and themes.
   - Interview senior leadership to extract goals and clarify intent.

2. **Define goals and objectives**
   - Capture high-level goals as desired outcomes or end states.
   - Capture objectives as specific, measurable, actionable targets.
   - Apply the SMART criteria to refine objectives.

3. **Choose the representation**
   - Use a tabular Goal/Objective Matrix when the focus is on alignment and
     clarity.
   - Use a graphical Goal/Objective Diagram when the emphasis is on narrative
     flow and visual strategy communication.

4. **Build the map or diagram**
   - In a matrix, place goals in rows and objectives in columns, marking the
     intersections that represent alignment.
   - In a diagram, draw goals as nodes and connect them to supporting objectives
     with lines or arrows.

5. **Validate the relationships**
   - Check for objectives that do not relate to any goal.
   - Check for goals without clear supporting objectives.
   - Ensure every objective is measurable and every goal is supported.

6. **Extend the strategy path**
   - Optionally add drivers above goals or initiatives below objectives to show
     the broader strategy-to-execution chain.

7. **Review and store**
   - Validate with senior management and strategy sponsors.
   - Store the map in the Architecture Repository for reuse and governance.

---

## Decision points and guidance

- **Matrix vs diagram**: prefer a matrix for alignment checking and a diagram for
  storytelling.
- **SMART objectives**: refine objectives until they are specific, measurable,
  assignable, realistic, and time-bound.
- **Goal definition**: keep goals outcome-oriented rather than action-oriented.
- **Relationship mapping**: show only meaningful linkages to avoid clutter.
- **Extension**: include drivers or initiatives when you want to show the full
  strategy-to-execution flow.

---

## Quality criteria

A strong Goal/Objective Diagram should be:

- **Strategically aligned**: objectives clearly support one or more goals.
- **Measurable**: objectives are stated in a way that supports progress tracking.
- **Readable**: the chosen format is easy to scan and understand.
- **Complete**: no major goals are unsupported, and no objectives are orphaned.
- **Valid**: reviewed and confirmed by senior leadership and stakeholders.
