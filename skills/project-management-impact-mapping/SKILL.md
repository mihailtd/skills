# Impact Mapping

This skill helps AI explain how to use Impact Mapping to align features and epics with business goals, surface assumptions, and prioritize the most valuable work.

## When to use this skill

Use this skill when you need to:

- discover which features really matter to business goals,
- translate a business goal into actors, impacts, and deliverables,
- prioritize work by the value it delivers and the assumptions it depends on,
- review an existing backlog to verify that features map to goals,
- explain how to use Impact Mapping in early discovery workshops.

## Outcome

Produce guidance that:

- defines the four key Impact Map layers: goal, actor, impact, and deliverable,
- shows how a single business goal can drive multiple feature ideas,
- teaches teams to think in terms of behavior change and business value,
- encourages naming assumptions explicitly and linking them to metrics,
- explains reverse Impact Mapping to validate a proposed backlog.

## Instructions for the AI

1. **Introduce the Impact Map structure**
   - Describe the goal as the business outcome to achieve.
   - Describe actors as the people or stakeholders who can help or hinder that goal.
   - Describe impact as the behavior change the actors need to make.
   - Describe deliverables as the capabilities or features that support that impact.

2. **Explain why it is useful**
   - Show that it makes the relationship between work and value visible.
   - Explain that it helps teams spot wasted effort early.
   - Emphasize that assumptions and metrics belong in the map.

3. **Walk through the conversation flow**
   - Pain point: why are we doing this?
   - Goal: what outcome do we want?
   - Actor: who matters and who can influence the outcome?
   - Impact: how should their behavior or experience change?
   - Deliverable: what should we build or do to support that change?

4. **Use an example narrative**
   - Illustrate with a business example such as a loyalty program or customer growth goal.
   - Explain how a goal like increasing ticket sales can lead to actors such as travelers and call center staff.
   - Show how impacts and deliverables flow from those actors and goals.

5. **Teach reverse Impact Mapping**
   - Explain how to start with a proposed feature set and work backward to goals and actors.
   - Use this reverse mapping to compare competing proposals and prioritize objectively.
   - Highlight that it is useful when stakeholders bring feature requests without a clear business case.

6. **Encourage measurable assumptions**
   - Recommend linking each impact or deliverable to a metric or success criterion.
   - Ask the team to surface risky assumptions and test them quickly.
   - Stress that the map should be updated as evidence accumulates.

## Decision points and guidance

- **If the user has a long feature list:** explain reverse Impact Mapping to validate and prioritize it.
- **If the team lacks business clarity:** focus on goal and actor discovery first.
- **If stakeholders are debating features:** recommend using the map as a conversation anchor.
- **If the team needs to track progress:** advise attaching metrics to map branches.

## Quality criteria

A strong Impact Mapping response should be:

- **structured:** it clearly describes goal, actors, impact, and deliverables,
- **value-focused:** it keeps business goals front and center,
- **assumption-aware:** it makes implicit bets explicit,
- **visual:** it encourages using a map or diagram,
- **prioritized:** it helps distinguish which deliverables matter most.

## Example prompts

- "How can we use Impact Mapping to prioritize our backlog?"
- "What is the right way to map features back to business goals?"
- "How do we compare conflicting feature requests using business value?"
