# Speculate Phase

This skill helps AI explain how to use the Speculate phase to discover high-level business goals, align product vision with measurable outcomes, and identify the right features before detailed implementation begins.

## When to use this skill

Use this skill when you need to:

- explain the role of the Speculate phase in BDD and agile planning,
- describe how strategic planning and backlog refinement work together,
- help teams connect vision, goals, capabilities, and features,
- prioritize early work based on business value and validated assumptions,
- avoid premature, rigid specification before the team understands the problem.

## Outcome

Produce guidance that:

- defines the Speculate phase as the discovery and prioritization stage,
- shows how it sits before the Illustrate phase and after initial business analysis,
- stresses that it is an ongoing activity, not a one-time event,
- explains why high-level stakeholders and delivery team members should collaborate,
- maps business vision to goals, capabilities, features, and refined backlog items.

## Instructions for the AI

1. **Describe the Speculate phase clearly**
   - Explain that Speculate is where teams identify the most promising work to solve business problems.
   - Emphasize that it should focus on hypotheses and outcomes, not on detailed implementation.
   - Describe it as the bridge between strategic planning and the product backlog.

2. **Highlight the business-driven mindset**
   - Show that the phase starts with a vision and ends with prioritized deliverables.
   - Explain that business value is expressed as goals such as increasing revenue, reducing costs, protecting value, or avoiding risk.
   - Encourage the team to think in terms of measurable outcomes and testable assumptions.

3. **Explain collaboration and participation**
   - Recommend involving senior stakeholders, product owners, developers, testers, and domain experts.
   - Explain that team members bring feasibility, quality, and technical insight to strategic decisions.
   - Note that this participation reduces misunderstandings and improves adaptability.

4. **Distinguish Strategic Planning from backlog refinement**
   - Describe Strategic Planning as identifying goals, opportunities, and candidate features.
   - Describe Product Backlog Refinement as breaking those candidate features into prioritized, more manageable items.
   - Explain that both happen repeatedly throughout the project lifecycle.

5. **Use the vision-goals-capabilities-features model**
   - Explain that a vision provides direction, goals define value, capabilities describe what users need to do, and features are what the team builds.
   - Use an example such as a loyalty program or frequent flyer project to illustrate the mapping.
   - Stress that features should always trace back to business goals.

6. **Warn against upfront detail and big requirements documents**
   - Explain that detailed spec documents created too early are likely to change and often waste effort.
   - Encourage teams to keep the initial work light and to focus on the business context.
   - Recommend revisiting priorities as new information emerges.

7. **Encourage continuous planning and learning loops**
   - Recommend two- to three-month cadences for many teams, but say shorter or longer cadences may be appropriate.
   - Explain that the Speculate phase should absorb learnings from delivered work and market changes.
   - Use business agility language: learn fast, adapt quickly, preserve quality.

## Decision points and guidance

- **If asked about feature delivery versus discovery:** keep the answer focused on understanding the problem and validating value before implementation.
- **If the team is mature in agile planning:** emphasize fast feedback cycles and continuous strategic review rather than one-shot planning.
- **If the user asks about prioritization:** recommend prioritizing by business goal alignment, risk reduction, and hypothesis validation.
- **If they ask about documentation:** recommend lightweight artifacts that support shared understanding, not heavyweight specification.

## Quality criteria

A strong Speculate-phase response should be:

- **business-focused:** it starts with vision and value, not technical solutions,
- **collaborative:** it makes stakeholder participation central,
- **hypothesis-aware:** it treats features as bets that need validation,
- **adaptive:** it frames planning as ongoing and responsive,
- **traceable:** it maps deliverables back to business goals.
